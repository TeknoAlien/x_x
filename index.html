Cybersecurity 101: Protect yourself online

[!] ALREADY COMPROMISED? [!]

Essential Safety Tips

Identity & contact information

  • Don't share your real email or social links publicly
  • Use aliasesAlternate names or email addresses that forward to your real inbox or throwaway addresses for forums, giveaways, and game servers
  • Use multiple emails: one primary for important accounts, separate ones for signups, gaming, and social
  • Use email aliases/forwarding services so you can disable an address if it gets spammed or traced
  • Don't put personal contact info in your public profile or Discord bio
  • Use different usernames for different platforms to prevent cross-referencingLinking your different accounts together to build a profile about you
  • Use a separate "burnerTemporary/disposable phone number you can throw away" number for signups (Google Voice, MySudo, Burner)
  • Enable WHOIS privacy on all domains you own - Without it, your name, email, phone, and address are publicly searchable. Most registrars offer privacy protection for $3-5/year
  • Limit social media exposure - Attackers research targets on social media. Don't post workplace details, vacation plans, family info, or location data. Set profiles to private

Media & metadata

  • Don't share photos that can be reverse-searchedUsing Google Images or TinEye to find where else a photo appears online or traced to your social accounts
  • Remove metadataHidden information embedded in files (location, date, device used) (EXIFExchangeable Image File Format - hidden data in photos that can reveal location, camera, date/time) and avoid posting the same photo across multiple platforms
  • Strip metadata before sharing documents/photos - Use ExifToolCommand-line tool to view and remove metadata from images and files or MAT2Metadata Anonymisation Toolkit - removes metadata from documents, images, and audio files to clean files. Photos contain location data, camera model, and timestamps
  • Don't send face pics or identifying media to people you don't fully trust

Authentication & credentials

  • Use a strong password manager + generator
  • Use a unique strong password for each account to prevent credential reuse attacks
  • Minimum password length: 16+ characters - Length matters more than complexity. Longer passwords exponentially increase cracking time
  • Consider passphrasesMultiple random words combined into a long memorable password, e.g., "correct-horse-battery-staple" for master passwords - easier to remember, harder to crack (e.g., "correct-horse-battery-staple" or "blue-mountain-coffee-sunrise")
  • Consider open-source password managers (e.g., BitwardenFree, open-source password manager with end-to-end encryption) for transparency
  • Never use password "hints" - they're searchable and often give away your password
  • Only change passwords after a confirmed breach - Routine password changes don't improve security and lead to weaker, predictable passwords
  • Never share passwords, even with family or trusted friends - use password manager sharing features if needed
  • Beware of fake password reset emails - always navigate to the site directly, never click email links
  • Enable 2FATwo-Factor Authentication - requires a second verification step beyond your password wherever possible (app/hardware tokens are more secure than SMS)
  • Avoid SMS-based 2FA when possible - use authenticator apps (Google Authenticator, Aegis, Authy) instead. SMS can be hijacked through SIM swappingAttack where attacker convinces your carrier to transfer your number to their SIM card attacks
  • Protect against SIM swapping - Contact your carrier to enable a carrier PIN/password on your account. This prevents attackers from transferring your number without the PIN
  • Never click links in text messages - SMS phishing (smishing) is common. Verify sender by calling the official number or navigating to the website directly
  • Don't reuse recovery emails or phone numbers across many accounts

Account recovery

  • Be careful with account recovery info—it can be abused if attackers know it
  • Keep recovery channels private and distinct
  • Use a secondary, dedicated recovery address not publicly connected to your identity

Email security

  • Use end-to-end encrypted email providers (ProtonMail) for sensitive communications
  • Never click links in unsolicited emails - navigate to sites directly by typing the URL
  • Check sender addresses carefully - attackers use look-alike domains (paypa1.com vs paypal.com)
  • Enable email SPFSender Policy Framework - validates that emails are sent from authorized servers, DKIMDomainKeys Identified Mail - cryptographically verifies email authenticity, and DMARCDomain-based Message Authentication - tells servers how to handle emails that fail authentication if you own a domain - prevents email spoofing
  • Be suspicious of urgent requests, especially those involving money or credentials
  • Verify sender identity through separate channel (call official number, not the one in email)
  • Use temporary email providers for signups you don't care about (Temp-Mail.org, Guerrilla Mail) - disposable addresses that don't require registration. Warning: you can't recover accounts if you lose access since the email is temporary
  • Disable automatic image loading in emails - tracking pixels report when you open emails
  • Don't reply to spam - confirms your email is active
  • Use separate email accounts for different purposes (personal, work, shopping, throwaway)

Downloads & executable content

  • Don't click unknown links—even in DMs or Discord
  • Treat verification or claiming links as suspicious
  • Don't download files from strangers
  • Avoid opening executablesPrograms that can run on your computer (.exe, .app, .dmg files), scriptsCode files that automate tasks (.bat, .sh, .py files), archives, or macrosAutomated commands in documents that can execute malicious code from unknown people
  • Never run commands/terminal scripts from untrusted sources
  • Do not log into other people's accounts (can reveal your IPInternet Protocol address - your device's unique identifier on the internet/location)
  • Don't authorize weird Discord bots or random OAuthOpen Authorization - allows apps to access your account without giving them your password flows
  • Avoid verifying accounts via links from bots—some are IP loggersMalicious links that capture your IP address when you click them

Platform & community safety

  • Use different usernames for each platform to limit cross-platform tracking
  • Don't put other users or personal people in your profile
  • Don't link your Spotify, Steam, or any other social accounts to your Discord profile
  • Don't share your Spotify playlists or profile—it can be used to identify you
  • Be cautious about community content (modsModifications - user-created add-ons that change how a game works, pluginsSoftware components that add specific features to existing programs) for games
  • Only download mods from official, trusted sources (CurseForge, Modrinth for Minecraft, Steam Workshop for Steam games, Nexus Mods for verified content)
  • Avoid joining private or unknown Minecraft servers, as server hosts can see your IP address.
  • Only download from known, reputable authors and always check the comments section for warnings or issues
  • Verify if mod installers ask to run scripts or external installers
  • Use VirusTotalFree online service that scans files with 70+ antivirus engines or MetaDefenderMulti-scanner that checks files for malware using multiple antivirus engines to scan suspicious files and links, but don't fully trust them—they only give a rough idea and aren't 100% accurate
  • To hide your email from appearing in GitHub commitsSaved changes in code repositories that show who made the change, go to Settings → Emails and enable "Keep my email addresses private"—this only applies to future commits, not previous ones, so be aware.

Network & browsing privacy

  • Use Brave with uBlock OriginPrivacy-focused ad blocker that blocks trackers and malicious scripts for maximum privacy
  • Use a reputable VPNVirtual Private Network - encrypts your internet traffic and hides your IP address (e.g., MullvadPrivacy-focused VPN that accepts crypto payments and requires no personal info, buy with crypto for increased privacy) to hide your IP
  • VPNs add a layer of privacy but won't make you invincible
  • Always check a VPN's no-logs policy and jurisdiction
  • Never use public WiFi without a VPN—cafes, airports, hotels are dangerous. Attackers can intercept your traffic, steal passwords, and see what you're doing

Browser privacy & tracking prevention

  • Use privacy-focused browsers with fingerprint resistanceProtection against websites tracking you by your unique browser/device characteristics (Tor BrowserAnonymous browser that routes traffic through multiple servers to hide your identity, LibreWolf, Brave)
  • Disable WebRTCWeb Real-Time Communication - can leak your real IP address even when using a VPN to prevent IP leaks even when using a VPN
  • Use container tabsBrowser feature that isolates websites from each other to prevent tracking to isolate different activities and prevent cross-site tracking
  • Clear cookiesSmall data files websites store on your device to remember you and cacheTemporary storage of web pages to load them faster regularly, or use automatic cleaning on browser close
  • Disable third-party cookies completely in browser settings
  • Use privacy-respecting search engines (DuckDuckGo, Brave Search)
  • Disable JavaScriptProgramming language that makes websites interactive but can be used for tracking on untrusted sites (use NoScript or similar)
  • Resist the urge to install browser extensions—each one increases your fingerprint

Device & hardware security

  • Use full-disk encryptionEncrypting your entire hard drive so data is unreadable if device is stolen (VeraCryptFree, open-source disk encryption software - encrypts your entire drive to protect data if device is stolen for Windows, FileVault for Mac, LUKS for Linux)
  • Cover your webcam when not in use or use a physical privacy shutter
  • Disable microphone access for apps and services you don't fully trust
  • Enable file extension visibility in File Explorer - Malware often disguises itself as harmless files (e.g., "invoice.pdf.exe" looks like "invoice.pdf" if extensions are hidden). Windows: File Explorer → View → Show file extensions. Mac: Finder → Preferences → Show all filename extensions
  • Never use public USB charging stations (airports, hotels, cafes) - they can install malware or steal data (juice jacking). Use your own charger and wall outlet, or bring a power bank
  • Never plug in unknown USB drives - they can contain malware that auto-runs when inserted. Treat all found USBs as hostile
  • Disable USB auto-run features on your computer to prevent automatic malware execution
  • Consider using a separate device for sensitive activities (banking, private communications)
  • Keep your OSOperating System - the main software that runs your computer (Windows, macOS, Linux) and all software updated with the latest security patchesUpdates that fix security vulnerabilities in software
  • Disable location services when not needed—many apps track you unnecessarily
  • Turn off Bluetooth and WiFi when not actively using them
  • Use a strong BIOS/UEFIBasic Input/Output System - firmware that starts your computer before the operating system loads password to prevent boot-level attacks
  • Enable automatic screen lock after short idle periods
  • For maximum security, consider using Tails OSPrivacy-focused operating system that runs from USB and leaves no trace on the computer (live operating system that leaves no traces)
  • Close unnecessary ports - audit yourself with Nmap - Open ports are entry points for attackers. Scan your own system with NmapNetwork mapper - powerful tool to scan for open ports and services on a network to see what's exposed, then close unused ports
  • Use firewall to block unused services - Enable Windows Firewall, UFW (Linux), or pfSense. Block all incoming connections except what you need. Default deny, explicit allow
  • Disable service banners that reveal software versions - Configure web servers (Apache/Nginx) to hide version numbers in HTTP headers. Attackers use version info to find exploits
  • Use intrusion detection systems for advanced users - SnortOpen-source intrusion detection system that monitors network traffic for suspicious activity or SuricataHigh-performance network IDS/IPS that detects and prevents intrusions can detect reconnaissance attempts and attacks in real-time

Router & IoT security

  • Change default router password immediately - Default credentials are publicly known. Use strong unique password for router admin panel
  • Never expose IoTInternet of Things - smart devices connected to internet (cameras, thermostats, speakers, locks, etc.) devices directly to internet - Smart cameras, thermostats, and speakers should never be accessible from outside your network. Use VPN for remote access
  • Put IoT devices on separate network - Create guest/isolated VLAN for smart devices so they can't access your main computers if compromised
  • Disable UPnP on router - UPnPUniversal Plug and Play - allows devices to automatically open ports, creating security risks automatically opens ports and creates security holes. Manually configure port forwarding only when needed
  • Change default credentials on ALL IoT devices - Smart cameras, NAS drives, printers - everything has default passwords that attackers know
  • Update router firmware regularly - Routers are rarely updated but contain critical vulnerabilities. Check manufacturer site quarterly
  • Disable WPS on router - WPSWiFi Protected Setup - allows easy connection but has major security flaws that can be exploited has known vulnerabilities that allow attackers to crack WiFi passwords in hours
  • Use WPA3 encryption if available - Upgrade from WPA2. If router doesn't support WPA3, at minimum use WPA2-AES (never WPA or WEP)
  • Disable remote management on router unless absolutely necessary
  • Review connected devices regularly - Unknown device = potential intruder
  • Consider using custom router firmware (OpenWrtOpen-source router firmware with latest security patches and active development - recommended over DD-WRT recommended) for advanced security features - OpenWrt uses latest LTS kernel while DD-WRT uses older EOL kernels with known vulnerabilities

Digital footprint management

  • Regularly audit your privacy settings on all platforms—they change frequently
  • Limit who can see your posts, friends list, and personal information
  • Don't tag your location in real-time—wait until after you've left
  • Review and revoke app permissions regularly (especially third-party apps)
  • Google yourself periodically to see what information is publicly available about you
  • Request data deletion from old accounts you no longer use
  • Use ProtonMail instead of Gmail for better privacy
  • Don't post information that reveals your daily routines or patterns
  • Be careful about "fun" quizzes and personality tests—they often harvest data
  • Disable people tagging you in photos without approval

Data & file security

  • Avoid cloud storage whenever possible—store sensitive files locally with encryptionConverting data into unreadable code that only you can decrypt (VeraCrypt)
  • If you must use cloud, use Proton Drive or self-hostRun your own server/storage instead of using third-party services your own solution
  • Follow the 3-2-1 backup rule: Keep 3 copies of your data (1 primary + 2 backups), on 2 different types of media (e.g., internal drive + external HDD + USB), with 1 copy stored offsite (physically separate location or secure cloud)
  • Prefer physical encrypted backups over cloud - Use external HDDs or SSDs with hardware encryptionBuilt-in encryption chip in the drive itself - no software needed or VeraCrypt encryption. Cloud backups can be accessed by providers, law enforcement, or hackers
  • Test your backups regularly - Many people discover their backups are corrupted only when they need them. Verify you can actually restore files every few months
  • Air-gapped backups for ransomware protection - Keep one backup completely disconnected from your computer (physically unplug external drive when not backing up). Ransomware cannot encrypt offline drives
  • Use versioned backups/snapshots - Keep multiple versions of files so you can restore from before corruption or ransomware encryption
  • Encrypt backup drives with strong passwords - If drive is stolen, data remains protected
  • Securely delete files—don't just delete them (use secure erase tools like BleachBit, Eraser)
  • Don't screenshot or save sensitive info in unencrypted notes apps
  • Use password-protected archivesCompressed files (.zip, .7z) that bundle multiple files together (7-Zip with AES-256Advanced Encryption Standard - military-grade encryption algorithm) for sensitive file sharing
  • Verify file integrity with checksumsUnique fingerprint of a file to verify it hasn't been tampered with when downloading important files
  • Don't store passwords, private keysSecret codes used to decrypt encrypted data or access crypto wallets, or sensitive documents in plain text

Operational security (OPSECOperations Security - protecting sensitive information through careful behavior and procedures)

  • Don't discuss sensitive plans or activities on monitored or public platforms
  • Assume everything you type online is permanent and could be leaked or subpoenaed
  • Don't mix personas—keep different online identities completely separate (accounts, devices, browsers)
  • Be aware of time zone leaks in posts, timestamps, and metadata
  • Watch out for background details in photos: reflections, documents, street signs, locations
  • Don't announce when you're away from home, on vacation, or traveling
  • Vary your patterns—don't be predictable in login times, locations, or online activity
  • Use different writing styles for different personas to avoid correlation
  • Be cautious about what you "like" or interact with—it creates a profile of your interests
  • Don't reuse unique phrases, quotes, or expressions across different identities
  • Think twice before trusting anyone—people can change in an instant. Human error is the greatest vulnerability. The human factor is often your biggest enemy.

Incident response (breached/rattedInfected with a RAT (Remote Access Trojan) that gives attackers control of your device)

  • Immediately disconnect from the internet (unplug ethernet, disable WiFi)
  • Do NOT log into any accounts from the compromised device
  • Change all passwords from a DIFFERENT, clean device
  • Enable 2FA on all accounts if you haven't already
  • Check for unauthorized logins and active sessions on all accounts
  • Revoke all active sessions and API tokensAccess keys that allow apps to use your account without your password/access keys
  • Scan the compromised device with multiple antivirusSoftware that detects and removes malware/viruses/anti-malware tools (Malwarebytes, Kaspersky, HitmanPro, Windows Defender Offline)
  • Check startup programs, scheduled tasksAutomated programs set to run at specific times, and browser extensions for suspicious entries
  • If RAT/malware persists, completely wipe and reinstall your operating system
  • Monitor your financial accounts for unauthorized transactions
  • Consider freezing your creditPreventing anyone from opening new credit accounts in your name if personal information was exposed
  • Document everything—timestamps, suspicious activity, files accessed
  • Report to relevant platforms and authorities if necessary
  • Notify contacts that your account may have been compromised (they might receive malicious messages)
  • Review what data was accessible on the compromised device and assume it's all stolen

General awareness & mindset

  • Minimize personal details you share: hometown, workplace, school, family, birthdate, etc.
  • Every detail is another link for someone investigating you
  • If you think you're targeted, preserve evidence and consider changing keys/passwords
  • Consult professionals if needed

Common Cyber Attacks

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts a victim's files, with attackers demanding a ransom to restore access.

Phishing / Social Engineering

Deceptive emails or messages designed to steal sensitive data like passwords or credit card numbers.

DDoS Attacks

Distributed Denial of Service: Flooding a system with traffic to overwhelm resources and crash services.

Man-in-the-Middle (MitM)

Intercepting communication between two parties to steal information.

Insider Threats

Security risks originating from within the organization, such as employees or contractors.

SQL Injection

Injecting malicious code into a server to view or steal data from a database.

Zero-Day Exploits

Attacks targeting unknown software vulnerabilities before a patch is released.

Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Targeted phishing scams aimed at employees to authorize fraudulent bank transfers.

Credential Theft

Using stolen usernames and passwords to gain unauthorized access.

SIM Swapping

Convincing your phone carrier to transfer your number to a new SIM card to gain account access.

Keylogger

Software that records everything you type to steal passwords and sensitive data.

Spyware

Software installed to monitor device activity, location, and communications—often by someone you know.

RAT (Remote Access Trojan)

Malware that gives attackers full remote control of your device, allowing them to spy, steal data, or execute commands.

Cryptojacking

Using your device's resources to mine cryptocurrency without your permission or knowledge.

IP Grabbing

Capturing your IP address on peer-to-peer (P2P) platforms where users connect directly to each other (Skype calls, some games) using tools like Wireshark. Discord uses centralized servers—all traffic goes through Discord's servers, not directly between users—so IP grabbing cannot happen on Discord.

Emerging Threats

  • AI-Powered Attacks: Hackers using Artificial Intelligence to create sophisticated phishing messages and bypass defenses.
  • Supply Chain Attacks: Compromising a trusted vendor to gain access to their customers' systems.
  • Cloud-Based Attacks: Targeting misconfigured cloud storage and services to steal data.
